# 1.5 - Transmission Media and Transceivers

Textbook source: Chapter 5, *Cabling Solutions and Issues*.

Transmission media are the physical or wireless paths that carry network signals. The exam expects you to compare speed, distance, connector, environment, and use case.

## Baseband, Broadband, and Duplex

Baseband uses the medium for a single digital signal. Ethernet LANs are normally treated as baseband.

Broadband divides capacity into channels and can carry multiple signals over the same medium. Cable-provider networks are a common example.

Simplex communication is one direction only. Half-duplex allows both directions, but only one side transmits at a time. Full-duplex allows simultaneous sending and receiving. Modern switched Ethernet normally uses full duplex.

## Copper Media

Twisted-pair copper is the familiar Ethernet cable family. It uses pairs of wires twisted together to reduce interference. UTP is unshielded twisted pair. STP includes shielding for noisier environments.

Ethernet cable categories describe performance capability. Higher categories generally support higher speeds or better signal characteristics, but the standard, distance, and equipment must all match.

Coaxial cable has a central conductor, insulation, shielding, and an outer jacket. It resists attenuation and interference better than simple copper pairs in some environments and is common in cable broadband contexts.

Direct attach copper uses twinaxial cable for short, high-speed links, often within or between nearby racks.

Plenum cable is rated for spaces used for air circulation. It uses materials that produce less toxic smoke. PVC/non-plenum cable is not appropriate where plenum-rated cabling is required.

## Fiber Media

Fiber uses light instead of electrical signals. It is resistant to electromagnetic interference and supports long distances.

Multimode fiber supports shorter distances and is common inside buildings and campuses. Single-mode fiber supports longer distances and uses a narrower light path.

Fiber can use different wavelengths and optics. The optic and fiber type must match the required speed and distance.

## Wireless Media

Wireless media uses radio rather than cable. For Domain 1, recognize 802.11 wireless, cellular, and satellite as transmission options. Wireless is easier to deploy where cables are impractical, but it is more exposed to interference, distance limits, and environmental factors.

## Connectors

RJ45 is the standard Ethernet twisted-pair connector. RJ11 is associated with telephone connections. F-type connectors are common with coaxial cable television/broadband. BNC is another coaxial connector used in some networking and video contexts.

Fiber connectors include SC, LC, ST, and MPO. LC is small and common on modern transceivers. SC is larger and uses a push-pull style. ST uses a twist-lock style. MPO carries multiple fibers in one connector and is common in high-density fiber environments.

## Transceivers

A transceiver converts between a network device and the transmission medium. SFP and QSFP are modular transceiver form factors. They are not just "plugs"; they must match speed, media type, wavelength, distance, and protocol.

Ethernet and Fibre Channel are different protocol contexts. A transceiver that physically fits is not automatically correct for the network role.

## Wiring and Cable Types

TIA/EIA 568A and 568B define twisted-pair pinouts. A straight-through cable uses the same pinout on both ends. A crossover cable uses different pinouts to connect like devices in older scenarios. Modern auto-MDI/MDIX reduces the need for crossover cables, but the concept still appears.

A rollover cable is used for console connections. A loopback cable or adapter feeds transmit back to receive for testing.

Media couplers join similar media. Media converters translate between different media, such as copper and fiber.

## Ethernet Standards and Distance

Ethernet copper and fiber standards define the expected speed, medium, and distance. A question that gives a distance requirement is usually asking you to eliminate media that cannot reach far enough.

Speed alone is not enough. You need the right medium, the right connectors, the right transceivers, and the right standard for the distance.

## Multiplexing

Multiplexing allows multiple signals to share a medium. Time-division multiplexing separates signals by time slots. Frequency-division and wavelength-division approaches separate signals by frequency or light wavelength. Dense and coarse wavelength-division multiplexing are fiber examples.

## Quick Comparison

| Need | Good answer |
|---|---|
| Long distance, EMI resistance | Fiber |
| Short high-speed rack connection | DAC/twinax |
| Common office Ethernet | Twisted-pair copper |
| Cable broadband style medium | Coax |
| Air-handling space | Plenum-rated cable |
| Modular fiber/copper optic | SFP/QSFP transceiver |

## Copper Cable Categories

The textbook's cable discussion supports a practical exam habit: match the cable to the speed and distance. Category-rated twisted-pair cabling improves over generations. A higher category can support better performance, but only if the installed cable, connectors, patch panels, and equipment all support the desired standard.

Do not choose a cable only because it has a familiar connector. RJ45 describes the connector style used with twisted-pair Ethernet, not the cable's full performance.

## Interference and Environment

Copper can be affected by electromagnetic interference and crosstalk. Shielded twisted pair can help in noisy environments. Fiber is immune to electromagnetic interference because it uses light, not electrical signaling.

The environment also affects jacket choice. Plenum-rated cable is required in air-handling spaces because fire and smoke behavior matters. Outdoor or industrial runs may require different protection from moisture, temperature, or physical damage.

## Fiber Distance Thinking

Multimode fiber is usually chosen for shorter distances, such as inside a building or campus. Single-mode fiber is usually chosen for long distances. The transceiver optic matters because the light source and wavelength must fit the fiber and distance.

If the question emphasizes long distance and high immunity to interference, fiber rises to the top. If it emphasizes short rack-to-rack high-speed connectivity, DAC/twinax may be the better answer.

## Connector Recognition

RJ45 belongs with Ethernet twisted-pair. RJ11 belongs with telephone-style cabling. F-type and BNC belong with coaxial contexts. SC, LC, ST, and MPO belong with fiber.

MPO is especially tied to multi-fiber, high-density use. LC is common on modern SFP-style fiber connections. ST is older and twist-lock. SC is larger and push-pull.

## Transceiver Selection

Transceivers must match form factor, speed, media, wavelength, and protocol. An SFP slot does not mean any SFP is correct. A short-reach multimode optic is not a long-haul single-mode optic. A Fibre Channel optic and an Ethernet optic may not be appropriate substitutes even if the module shape looks similar.

This is a common real-world pain point, and exams love real-world pain with tidy answer choices.

## Cable Construction and Testing Context

Straight-through cables connect unlike devices in traditional Ethernet logic. Crossover cables connect like devices in older scenarios. Rollover cables are console cables. Loopback adapters are testing tools.

Even though troubleshooting belongs mainly to Domain 5, these terms are also media concepts. You need to know what the physical item is before you can troubleshoot it.
