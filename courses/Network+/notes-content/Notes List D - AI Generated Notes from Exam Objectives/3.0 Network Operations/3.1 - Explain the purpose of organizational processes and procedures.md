# 3.1 - Organizational Processes and Procedures

> **Big idea:** Network operations are not only commands and devices. Procedures keep changes controlled, outages understandable, assets tracked, and teams aligned. The point is to reduce chaos while still letting the network evolve.

## What you should be able to explain

- Explain change management and maintenance windows.
- Understand documentation, diagrams, baselines, and inventories.
- Recognize incident, problem, and configuration management ideas.
- Connect procedures to risk reduction.

## Concept map

| Process | Purpose | Good evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Change management | Controls risk during modifications | Request, approval, plan, rollback |
| Incident management | Restores service quickly | Ticket, timeline, impact, resolution |
| Problem management | Finds root cause behind repeated incidents | RCA, known error, permanent fix |
| Asset management | Tracks equipment and lifecycle | Inventory, owner, location, support status |
| Configuration management | Tracks intended device/service state | Backups, versions, templates |

## Documentation that actually helps

- Physical diagrams show racks, cabling, and site layout.
- Logical diagrams show VLANs, routing, firewall zones, and dependencies.
- IP address management prevents duplicate assignments and mystery subnets.
- A good runbook explains how to perform a task safely, not just what the final state should be.

## Change control

- A change plan should describe scope, risk, test steps, communication, and rollback.
- A maintenance window gives users and teams a predictable time for disruption.
- Emergency changes may skip normal timing but should still be documented after the fact.
- Peer review catches mistakes before the network does.

## Baselines

- A baseline captures normal performance, utilization, and behavior.
- Without a baseline, "slow" is hard to prove.
- Baselines help capacity planning and anomaly detection.

## Scenario walkthrough

1. A switch firmware upgrade is needed at a busy site.
2. A good process includes the reason, affected devices, backup config, expected outage, test plan, rollback plan, and user notification.
3. The technical command is only one part of a successful change.

## Exam cues

- Change management reduces risk; it is not just paperwork.
- Incident response restores service; problem management asks why it happened.
- Current diagrams are more valuable than beautiful outdated diagrams.
