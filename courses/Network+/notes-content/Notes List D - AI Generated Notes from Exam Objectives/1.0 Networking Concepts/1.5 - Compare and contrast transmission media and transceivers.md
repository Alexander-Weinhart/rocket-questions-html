# 1.5 - Transmission Media and Transceivers

> **Big idea:** Transmission media are the physical paths that carry network signals. Copper uses electrical signaling, fiber uses light, and wireless uses radio. Transceivers convert device interfaces into the signaling needed for the selected medium.

## What you should be able to explain

- Compare copper, fiber, and wireless media.
- Understand connector and transceiver roles.
- Explain speed, distance, interference, and cost tradeoffs.
- Recognize common cabling and fiber terms.

## Concept map

| Medium | Strengths | Limitations | Common use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Twisted-pair copper | Inexpensive, common, supports PoE | Shorter distance, EMI concerns | Access switches to endpoints |
| Coaxial | Shielded and durable for certain systems | Less common in modern LAN switching | Cable internet, legacy or specialty links |
| Multimode fiber | High speed, immune to EMI, good campus distance | Shorter than single-mode | Data centers and building backbones |
| Single-mode fiber | Very long distance and high capacity | Optics can cost more | WAN, provider, long campus runs |
| Wireless | Mobility and flexible deployment | Shared medium, interference, security planning | Client access and point-to-point links |

## Copper concepts

- Twisted pairs reduce electromagnetic interference by balancing signal behavior.
- Shielded cable can help in noisy environments, but grounding and installation quality matter.
- PoE sends power and data over the same cable to devices such as access points, phones, and cameras.
- Cable category affects supported speeds and distances.

## Fiber concepts

- Fiber is immune to electromagnetic interference because it carries light instead of electrical current.
- Multimode fiber uses a larger core and is common for shorter high-speed links.
- Single-mode fiber uses a smaller core and supports much longer distances.
- Dirty connectors and incorrect optics are common real-world fiber problems.

## Transceivers

- SFP, SFP+, QSFP, and similar modules let a device support different media and speeds.
- The optic must match the fiber type, wavelength, speed, and distance requirement.
- Direct attach copper can be used for short high-speed connections between nearby devices.

## Scenario walkthrough

1. A switch uplink must cross a building where electrical interference is high and the distance is beyond normal copper limits.
2. Fiber is the better fit because it handles longer distance and avoids EMI.
3. The design must match fiber type, connector type, and transceiver requirements on both ends.

## Exam cues

- Copper is common and cheap, but distance and EMI matter.
- Fiber is not one thing. Single-mode and multimode are different design choices.
- A link can fail even with the right cable if the optic type or speed is wrong.
