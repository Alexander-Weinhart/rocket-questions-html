# 4.2 - Attacks and Their Network Impact

> **Big idea:** Network attacks try to disrupt service, steal information, bypass controls, or gain position for later movement. Understanding the attack pattern helps you choose useful prevention, detection, and response controls.

## What you should be able to explain

- Recognize DoS/DDoS, VLAN hopping, MAC flooding, spoofing, poisoning, rogue services, malware, and social engineering.
- Explain how attacks affect confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
- Match attacks with practical mitigations.
- Avoid confusing similar Layer 2 and Layer 3 attacks.

## Concept map

| Attack | What happens | Likely impact | Mitigation idea |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| DoS/DDoS | Service is overwhelmed | Availability loss | Filtering, rate limiting, scrubbing, capacity |
| MAC flooding | Switch CAM table is exhausted | Traffic leakage or instability | Port security |
| VLAN hopping | Attacker reaches unauthorized VLAN | Segmentation bypass | Disable DTP, set trunks explicitly, native VLAN care |
| ARP poisoning | False IP-to-MAC mapping | Interception or disruption | DAI, static entries where appropriate |
| DNS poisoning | False name resolution | Traffic redirection | DNSSEC, secure resolvers, monitoring |
| Rogue DHCP | Bad IP settings supplied | Traffic hijack or outage | DHCP snooping |

## Layer 2 attacks

- Layer 2 attacks often target trust inside a local network.
- MAC flooding abuses switch learning behavior.
- ARP poisoning abuses address resolution trust.
- VLAN hopping abuses trunking or tagging mistakes.
- Switch security features reduce these risks when configured correctly.

## Service and infrastructure attacks

- DoS and DDoS attacks reduce availability by exhausting bandwidth, state tables, CPU, or application resources.
- Rogue services such as unauthorized DHCP or DNS can redirect or break clients.
- Credential attacks may not look like network attacks at first, but they often lead to unauthorized access.

## Human-centered attacks

- Phishing and social engineering bypass technical controls by tricking people.
- Training helps, but technical controls such as MFA, filtering, and least privilege are still needed.
- Assume one layer can fail and design the next layer to limit damage.

## Scenario walkthrough

1. Clients suddenly receive a strange gateway and cannot reach internal resources after someone plugs in a small router.
2. That points to rogue DHCP.
3. DHCP snooping and port controls can prevent untrusted switch ports from handing out addresses.

## Exam cues

- ARP poisoning targets IP-to-MAC trust; DNS poisoning targets name resolution.
- DDoS is availability-focused.
- Many Layer 2 protections live on switches, not firewalls.
