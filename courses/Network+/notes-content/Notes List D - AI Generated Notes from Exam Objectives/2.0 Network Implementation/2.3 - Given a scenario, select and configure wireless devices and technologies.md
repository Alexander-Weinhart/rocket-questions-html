# 2.3 - Wireless Devices and Technologies

> **Big idea:** Wireless networking replaces a cable with shared radio space. That makes design choices such as frequency band, channel width, authentication, antenna placement, roaming, and interference management just as important as IP settings.

## What you should be able to explain

- Compare 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz behavior.
- Understand SSIDs, BSSIDs, channels, channel width, and roaming.
- Explain wireless security choices such as WPA2, WPA3, and enterprise authentication.
- Recognize design issues involving coverage, capacity, and interference.

## Concept map

| Band | Strengths | Limitations | Design note |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2.4 GHz | Longer range and better wall penetration | Fewer non-overlapping channels and more interference | Use carefully for legacy/IoT needs |
| 5 GHz | More channels and better capacity | Shorter range than 2.4 GHz | Common primary enterprise band |
| 6 GHz | More clean spectrum and high capacity | Requires newer clients and WPA3 in many contexts | Good for modern high-density designs |

## Coverage versus capacity

- Coverage asks whether the signal reaches the client.
- Capacity asks whether enough airtime exists for all clients to perform well.
- A network can show strong bars and still perform badly if too many clients share the same channel.
- Channel planning and transmit power matter.

## Security modes

- Personal mode uses a shared passphrase and is simpler for small environments.
- Enterprise mode uses 802.1X with a RADIUS-backed identity flow and is stronger for managed organizations.
- WPA3 improves security compared with older modes, but client compatibility must be checked.
- Open networks should use captive portals or other controls only when the risk is understood.

## Roaming

- Roaming is the client moving between access points.
- Controllers and standards can help, but the client ultimately makes many roaming decisions.
- Bad roaming design can cause sticky clients, voice drops, and uneven AP load.

## Scenario walkthrough

1. A classroom has good signal strength but video calls still freeze when full.
2. That points more toward capacity, channel contention, or interference than simple coverage.
3. Reducing channel width, adding APs carefully, or moving capable clients to cleaner bands may help.

## Exam cues

- 2.4 GHz is not automatically bad, but it is crowded and limited.
- Enterprise authentication usually points toward 802.1X/RADIUS.
- Wireless troubleshooting should consider RF conditions, not just IP settings.
