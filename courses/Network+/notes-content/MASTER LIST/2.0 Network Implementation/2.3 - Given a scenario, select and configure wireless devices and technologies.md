# 2.3 - Wireless Devices and Technologies

## Core Idea

Wireless networking depends on radio planning, channel use, standards, security, client association behavior, and access-point design.

## Channels

### Channel Width

- Wider channels can provide higher throughput.
- They also consume more spectrum and can increase overlap risk.

### Non-overlapping Channels

- Important for reducing interference and contention.
- Especially important in crowded environments.

### Regulatory Impacts

- Region-specific rules affect allowed frequencies, power, and channel behavior.

#### 802.11h

- Supports regulatory and spectrum-management behavior in certain environments.

## Frequency Options

### 2.4 GHz

- Longer reach and better obstacle penetration.
- More crowded and more prone to interference.

### 5 GHz

- More available channels and higher performance opportunities than 2.4 GHz in many cases.

### 6 GHz

- Newer spectrum with more bandwidth opportunities where supported.
- Requires compatible infrastructure and clients.

### Band Steering

- Encourages capable clients toward a preferred band.
- Helps improve client distribution and performance.

## SSID Concepts

### SSID

- Service Set Identifier is the wireless network name users recognize.

### BSSID

- Basic Service Set Identifier is the identifier for a specific AP radio cell, often tied to a MAC address.

### ESSID

- Extended service set identifier concept supports multiple APs presenting a consistent network experience.

## Network Types

### Mesh

- APs can form wireless backhaul relationships.

### Ad Hoc

- Peer-to-peer wireless without a central AP.

### Point-to-Point

- Directed link between two locations or devices.

### Infrastructure

- Standard AP-centered wireless design used by most organizations.

## Encryption

### WPA2

- Widely used Wi-Fi security standard.

### WPA3

- Newer Wi-Fi security standard with stronger improvements over WPA2.

## Guest Networks

### Captive Portals

- Web-based guest-authentication or acceptance pages often used before normal access is granted.

## Authentication

### PSK vs. Enterprise

- PSK uses a shared secret.
- Enterprise uses centralized identity-based authentication and is more scalable and secure for organizations.

## Antennas

### Omnidirectional

- Radiate broadly around the AP.

### Directional

- Focus radio energy in a preferred direction.

## Autonomous vs. Lightweight AP

- Autonomous APs are managed individually.
- Lightweight APs depend on central control for many functions.

## Big Picture

Wireless design is a balance of coverage, capacity, interference control, security, regulatory compliance, and user experience.
