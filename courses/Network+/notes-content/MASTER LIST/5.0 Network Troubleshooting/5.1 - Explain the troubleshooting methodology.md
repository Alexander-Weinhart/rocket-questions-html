# 5.1 - Troubleshooting Methodology

## Core Idea

A structured troubleshooting process reduces wasted effort, avoids assumptions, and improves recovery speed while preserving documentation and learning.

## 1. Identify the Problem

### Gather Information

- Collect symptoms, logs, observations, and scope details.

### Question Users

- Ask what happened, when it started, and what changed.

### Identify Symptoms

- Distinguish user perception from measurable behavior.

### Determine if Anything Has Changed

- Recent changes often reveal likely causes.

### Duplicate the Problem, If Possible

- Reproducing the issue helps verify symptoms and test fixes.

### Approach Multiple Problems Individually

- Separate issues rather than assuming they all share one cause.

## 2. Establish a Theory of Probable Cause

### Question the Obvious

- Do not ignore simple explanations.

### Consider Multiple Approaches

#### Top-to-bottom / Bottom-to-top OSI Model

- Start high or low in the stack and work through layers.

#### Divide and Conquer

- Test a strategic midpoint and narrow the issue from there.

## 3. Test the Theory

### If Confirmed

- Move to resolution planning.

### If Not Confirmed

- Form a new theory or escalate appropriately.

## 4. Establish a Plan of Action

- Decide what to change.
- Consider impact and side effects before acting.

## 5. Implement the Solution or Escalate

- Apply the fix if within your authority and skill.
- Escalate if needed.

## 6. Verify Full System Functionality

- Confirm not only that the immediate symptom is gone, but that normal operation is restored.
- Add preventive measures when appropriate.

## 7. Document Findings, Actions, Outcomes, and Lessons Learned

- Good documentation supports future troubleshooting and team learning.

## Big Picture

The methodology matters because random troubleshooting wastes time and increases risk. Process improves consistency and quality under pressure.
