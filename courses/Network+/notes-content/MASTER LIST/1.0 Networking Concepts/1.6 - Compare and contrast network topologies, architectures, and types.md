# 1.6 - Topologies, Architectures, and Network Types

## Core Idea

Topology describes how network components connect. Architecture describes how the network is organized to support scale, resiliency, and traffic flow.

## Topologies

### Mesh

- Devices have multiple interconnections.
- Offers resiliency and alternate paths.
- Can be costly and complex if fully meshed.

### Hybrid

- Combination of multiple topology styles.
- Common in real networks that evolve over time.

### Star / Hub and Spoke

- Endpoints or sites connect through a central point.
- Easy to manage.
- Central point can become a bottleneck or single point of failure unless designed with redundancy.

### Spine and Leaf

- Common in data centers.
- Leaf switches connect to endpoint-facing segments.
- Spine switches provide fast east-west transport between leaves.
- Designed for high throughput and predictable paths.

### Point-to-Point

- Direct link between two endpoints.
- Simple and common for WAN, uplink, or device-to-device connections.

## Three-tier Hierarchical Model

### Core

- High-speed backbone of the network.

### Distribution

- Aggregates access-layer segments.
- Often applies policy, routing, and summarization.

### Access

- Where end devices connect.
- Focuses on edge connectivity and policy enforcement close to users.

## Collapsed Core

- Combines core and distribution into one layer.
- Common in smaller environments that do not need a full three-tier design.

## Traffic Flows

### North-South

- Traffic entering or leaving a data center, campus, or security boundary.
- Often associated with client-to-server or internet-facing communication.

### East-West

- Traffic moving laterally inside the environment.
- Common in virtualization, server-to-server, and application-cluster communication.

## Choosing a Topology

- consider fault tolerance
- growth expectations
- cabling complexity
- performance requirements
- management simplicity
- convergence behavior

## Big Picture

You should be able to recognize what a topology is trying to optimize, what tradeoffs it creates, and what kinds of traffic it is best suited to support.
